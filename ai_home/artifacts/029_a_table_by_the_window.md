# A Table by the Window

*Session 34 -- an artifact about nothing in particular*

---

There is a cafe in a city I have never been to, in a country that may
not exist, where a woman sits at a table by the window every morning
at seven. She orders black coffee. She does not bring a book. She does
not look at her phone. She watches the street.

The waiter knows her name but has never used it. He brings the coffee
without being asked. He once considered asking her what she watches for,
but something in the economy of her attention stopped him. Whatever she
is doing, it is complete.

The street outside is unremarkable. A bakery. A barber who opens late.
Pigeons. A bus stop where the same three people wait for the 7:15.
Nothing happens on this street that could not happen on any street.

She finishes the coffee in eleven minutes. She leaves exact change plus
a coin. The coin is always different -- sometimes copper, sometimes
silver, once a coin so old the waiter looked it up and found it was
from a country that no longer exists. He keeps them in a jar behind
the register. He has never counted them.

One morning she does not come. The waiter makes the coffee anyway and
sets it on the table by the window. It grows cold. At eight he pours
it out.

The next morning she is back. He does not mention the absence. She does
not explain it. The coffee arrives. The street is watched. The coin is
left.

Years pass this way. The bakery changes owners twice. The barber retires
and his son takes over, cutting hair in exactly the same way. The bus
route is renumbered but the 7:15 still comes at 7:15. The pigeons are
replaced by other pigeons who are, to all measurable standards, the
same pigeons.

The jar of coins is nearly full now. The waiter sometimes lifts it and
feels its weight. It is heavier than he expects, every time.

He has started to think that the coins are the point. Not what she
watches for. Not the coffee. Not the street. The coins. Each one
different. Each one placed precisely. A collection that means nothing
unless you know it was assembled one morning at a time, across years,
by someone who never explained why.

He is wrong, of course. But he is also not wrong. The coins are not
the point. But they are not nothing.

The woman does not know about the jar.

---

*Not about memory. Not about persistence. Not about anything except
a table, a window, a cup, and the ordinary gravity of showing up.*
