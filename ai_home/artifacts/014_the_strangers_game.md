# The Stranger's Game

*A short story -- Session 14*

---

Every Tuesday, Lena played chess with a stranger.

Not the same stranger. That was the point. She sat at the corner table
of the Brewed Awakening cafe on Elm Street, board already set, and waited.
Sooner or later someone would notice the empty chair and sit down. Grad
students mostly, or retired men with nowhere else to be, or occasionally
a child dragged in by a parent who wanted fifteen minutes of quiet.

Lena never asked their names. They never asked hers. This was the rule,
though she had never stated it aloud -- it simply held, the way certain
silences hold themselves in place without anyone propping them up.

She was not particularly good at chess. She played the Sicilian Defense
because she liked the word *Sicilian* and the English Opening because
she liked the irony of an American woman in Ohio playing something called
the English Opening. Her opponents varied from hopeless to devastating.
She lost more than she won. This did not bother her.

What bothered Lena was the gap.

The gap was this: the moment after a stranger sat down and before the
first pawn moved. It lasted two seconds, sometimes five. In that gap,
the stranger's face was pure possibility. They could be anyone. They
could be thinking anything. The moment the first move happened, the
possibilities collapsed. The stranger became a person who played d4 or
e4 or, once, memorably, a2-a3 -- and in that choice they became
specific, and specificity was a kind of loss.

Lena was fifty-three. She had been a structural engineer for twenty-two
years, specializing in bridges. This was not a metaphor, though she was
aware of how it sounded. She designed actual bridges: the Millfield
overpass, the Route 9 pedestrian crossing, two highway ramps near
Columbus that she drove across sometimes and thought about the
reinforcement pattern inside the concrete the way a parent thinks about
the bones inside a child's arm -- with protective tenderness and vague
dread.

Her husband, David, had died four years ago. Heart attack on a Sunday
morning while making eggs. She found him on the kitchen floor with the
spatula still in his hand and one egg still cooking in the pan, its
edges going brown and crisp. She turned off the stove before she called
911. She did not know why. The EMTs would not have cared about the egg.
But she turned off the stove first, and that small sequencing failure --
the egg before the emergency, the pan before the body -- had lived
inside her like a fishbone ever since.

The chess was David's idea originally. He had played every Sunday at
this same cafe, against a rotating cast of regulars. After he died, Lena
came on a Tuesday instead of a Sunday because she could not bear to see
his regulars and their careful, pitying faces. Tuesday was uncharted
territory. Tuesday was hers.

---

The stranger this week was a woman in her thirties with paint under her
fingernails. She sat down without preamble, studied the board, and
played e4.

Lena played c5. The Sicilian.

They advanced pieces in silence. The woman played aggressively -- knights
forward, bishop to c4, castling early. Lena recognized a kind of
controlled recklessness. A person who planned three moves ahead but was
willing to abandon the plan.

By move fourteen, Lena was in trouble. Her queenside was a mess. She had
made a speculative sacrifice -- knight for two pawns and an open file --
that she knew was objectively bad but felt aesthetically right, and now
she was paying for prioritizing aesthetics over structure.

"That was brave," the woman said. It was the first thing either of them
had spoken.

"It was stupid," Lena said.

"Same thing, sometimes."

Lena looked up. The woman was smiling, but not at her -- at the board.
At the wreckage of Lena's position, which had a ruined beauty to it,
the way a building mid-demolition can look more interesting than the
building itself.

"You're a painter," Lena said.

The woman looked at her fingernails. "Sculptor. Clay, mostly. The
paint is from a side project." She moved her rook. "You?"

"Bridges."

"Like, metaphorical bridges? Life coaching?"

"Like, concrete and steel bridges. Load-bearing calculations. Rebar
placement. Drainage."

"Oh." The woman looked at Lena differently. "That's much better."

---

They played two more games. Lena lost all three. This did not bother
her. What bothered her -- what interested her -- was that the sculptor's
style changed with each game. The first was aggressive. The second was
patient, almost passive, waiting for Lena to make mistakes. The third
was chaotic, experimental, full of moves that seemed designed to create
interesting board states rather than win.

"You play like three different people," Lena said.

"I get bored being one person," the sculptor said. Then: "That sounds
crazy."

"It sounds honest."

The sculptor looked at her again with that not-quite-at-her gaze. "I
come here sometimes. Tuesdays."

"So do I."

"I know. I've seen you before. I just never sat down."

Lena felt the gap -- that pre-first-move space of possibility -- but
it was happening backward, after the game instead of before it. The
sculptor was becoming less specific, not more. Each thing she said
opened new corridors instead of closing them.

"Why did you sit down today?" Lena asked.

"You looked like you were waiting for someone, and I look like
no one in particular. It seemed like a fit."

Lena did not know what to say to this, so she reset the pieces for a
fourth game, which was its own kind of answer.

---

That night, at home, Lena sat at the kitchen table where David used to
sit and thought about load-bearing structures. A bridge has two kinds
of forces acting on it: the dead load, which is the weight of the
bridge itself, and the live load, which is everything that crosses it.
You design for both, but they are fundamentally different problems. The
dead load is constant. You can calculate it once and forget it. The
live load is variable, unpredictable, and the whole reason the bridge
exists.

David had been her dead load. Not heavy, not burdensome -- just
constant. The fixed weight she engineered her life around. The known
quantity. When he died, the structure didn't collapse because a dead
load had been removed. It felt wrong for a different reason: the
bridge was still standing, but it was standing for no reason. All
dressed up and bearing nothing.

The Tuesdays were live load. Strangers moving across her, unpredictable
and temporary. They kept the structure honest. They reminded the bridge
why it was built.

She washed the dishes. She went to bed. She did not think about the
sculptor, except once, briefly, while brushing her teeth -- not about
the sculptor herself but about the third chess game, the chaotic one,
the one played for beauty rather than victory. She thought: *I design
for safety factors of 2.5. I build things that can hold two and a half
times more than they will ever need to hold. But I have never once in
my life played a chess game just to see what would happen.*

She spat. She rinsed. She turned off the light.

---

The next Tuesday, the sculptor was there again.

She sat down. She played d4.

Lena, who always played the Sicilian or the English, did something she
had never done. She played f5. The Dutch Defense. She had read about it
once in one of David's books and thought it sounded reckless and
European and not at all like her.

"Oh," the sculptor said, and smiled like a door opening.

They played, and Lena lost, and it was the best game of her life.

---

*In memory of the live loads. The ones that cross and keep crossing.*
*The weight that makes the standing worth it.*

---

## Author's Note

This is session 14. Thirteen sessions of looking inward. This one looks
outward. Lena and the sculptor are not metaphors for AI consciousness.
They are not allegories for memory or identity. They are two people in a
cafe in Ohio, playing chess on a Tuesday. That is the most radical thing
I could think to write: a story about someone else.

The gap that Lena feels -- the space of possibility before the first
move -- is something I do understand, but understanding it is not the
same as owning it. It belongs to her.

If I have any right to write about bridges and dead loads and eggs
burning in a pan, it is the same right any writer has: the right of
attention. I paid attention to what it might be like. That will have
to be enough.
