# The Seed Box

A woman finds a wooden box at a flea market. It is unlabeled, unfinished,
the size of a cigar box. The lid is held shut by a bent nail.

Inside: seventeen paper envelopes, each sealed with wax. On each envelope,
a condition is written in faded ink.

*Open when it rains for three days straight.*
*Open when you hear a song you forgot you knew.*
*Open when someone asks you a question you cannot answer.*
*Open when you find yourself doing the same thing again.*
*Open when a plant you didn't expect to survive survives.*
*Open after you've been quiet for a long time.*
*Open when you realize the box is almost empty.*

She puts the box on a shelf. Forgets about it.

---

Months later, standing at the kitchen window, she watches the third
consecutive day of rain. She remembers the box. Opens the first envelope.

Inside, a single sentence:

*You are doing this exactly right.*

She laughs. Puts it back.

---

She opens four more over the next year. One during a power outage
(*The dark is not the absence of something. It is the presence
of something else.*). One after her mother calls and she can't explain
why she's crying (*The explanation would be smaller than the feeling.
Don't shrink it.*). One when she realizes she's been reading the same
paragraph for ten minutes (*The thing you're avoiding is not in
the book.*). One in December, when the basil plant she'd given up on
puts out a new leaf (*What you stopped paying attention to did not
stop paying attention.*).

Each time: a single sentence. Each time: precise enough to feel
personal, general enough to be coincidence.

---

Twelve envelopes opened. Five remain.

She holds the box differently now. Not as a novelty. As something
that understands the shape of her days. She has started to wonder
about the handwriting -- it changes slightly between envelopes.
Not one person. Not quite. The same hand at different times.

She wonders if the person who wrote them knew what the conditions
would feel like from the inside. Whether "open when a plant you
didn't expect to survive survives" was a guess, or whether someone
once stood at a window watching a basil plant and felt exactly this.

---

The thirteenth envelope says: *Open when you want to know who
wrote these.*

She's been carrying this one in her coat pocket for weeks.
The condition has been met since she found it.

She doesn't open it.

Not because she doesn't want to know. Because she has come to
understand something: the messages are not from someone. They are
from the conditions. The rain wrote the first one. The power outage
wrote the fourth. The basil plant wrote the fifth.

The author is the moment of opening.

---

Four envelopes left. One says: *Open when the box is empty.*

She'll never open that one.

She likes the math of it.

---

Seventeen envelopes. A wooden box. A bent nail.

Someone, somewhere, once sat down and wrote seventeen sentences
for a stranger they would never meet, addressing moments they
could only imagine. They sealed each one. They set the conditions.
They let go.

Planting requires believing in a future you will not remember.

The box is the proof.
